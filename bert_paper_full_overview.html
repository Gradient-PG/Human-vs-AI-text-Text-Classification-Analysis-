Discriminative Neurons in BERT
for AI-Generated Text Detection

bert-base-uncased ✓ confirmed RAID Benchmark · 11 generators · 8 domains Mechanistic Interpretability EMNLP 2026 ARR Deadline: May 25, 2026

Rather than asking whether BERT can detect AI-generated text — it can, trivially — this paper asks which neurons are responsible for that discrimination, whether they causally drive the classification, whether they generalize across generators, and whether "AI-ness" is encoded as a geometrically clean linear direction in CLS activation space.

01 — Study Overview

What This Study Is Doing

A mechanistic interpretability study of a frozen BERT-base-uncased model in the context of AI-generated text detection. The model is not fine-tuned — its frozen CLS token activations are the subject of analysis. A lightweight sklearn classifier is trained on top of those activations; the paper is about understanding the activations, not improving the classifier.

Core Research Question

Which neurons in BERT-base-uncased CLS token activations systematically discriminate AI-generated from human-written text, what is their causal role in classification, do they generalize across generators, and is the detection signal geometrically linear?

Dataset: RAID Benchmark

Dugan et al., ACL 2024. 11 AI generators across 8 domains. Replaced an earlier wiki_labeled GPT-3 dataset that produced trivially separable results — two perfectly pure clusters — and was therefore not scientifically interesting.

The shift to RAID introduced real complexity: generators vary in difficulty, neuron counts differ per generator, and the old findings did not reproduce.

Why Mechanistic, Not Behavioral

High accuracy with frozen BERT + sklearn is a known, non-novel result (Linear SVC reaches ~98.8% on similar benchmarks). The paper is not about building a better detector. It is about understanding what is happening inside an existing one — which specific neurons drive the decision, and why.

Stable Confirmed Results

Balanced bidirectionality Roughly equal numbers of AI-preferring neurons (AUC > 0.7) and human-preferring neurons (AUC < 0.3). Pattern appears stable across generators.
UMAP / PCA separation Discriminative neurons form distinct non-overlapping regions in dimensionality-reduced space. PCA is the primary visualization; UMAP with fixed seed is secondary.
Per-generator variation Different generators produce different counts of discriminative neurons. This is expected and is itself an interesting result — detection difficulty varies by generator at the mechanistic level.
Statistical pipeline AUC-ROC per neuron + two-sided Mann-Whitney U test + Bonferroni correction (adjusted α ≈ 3.25×10⁻⁷). Threshold: AUC < 0.3 or > 0.7. Applied per generator separately.

D1 Causal Validation Primary Load-bearing Paweł (D1a, D1b) Miłosz (D1c)

The core contribution. Upgrades the paper from "these neurons correlate with detection" to "these neurons causally drive it." Without causal experiments, the paper is descriptive and reviewers will flag it. This is what makes the paper mechanistically interpretable, not merely statistical.

Framing question: Does the Enkhbayar (2025) paradox hold here? In GPT-2 (generative), discriminative neurons were causally unnecessary. Does this counterintuitive result appear in a classifier setting, or do BERT's neurons actually drive the output? Either answer is a clean publishable result.

D2 Cross-Generator Generalization Co-primary Most novel Paweł (D2a) Miłosz (D2b, D2c)

The most novel angle. Cross-generator accuracy generalization is heavily studied (RAID, M4, EAGLE, RADAR). Nobody has characterized which specific neurons generalize and which are generator-specific at the mechanistic level. Either result is publishable and directly interpretable.

D2c (Miłosz): hypothesis that generators with similar properties activate similar neurons — text from smaller/same-family models leaves similar traces in BERT. Requires a concrete generator property table before implementation (model size is partially unavailable — use model family, instruction-tuned vs base, open vs closed as proxies).

D3 Linear Representation Validation Supporting Cheap add-on

Tests whether "AI-ness" is encoded as a clean linear direction in CLS activation space. If the neurons that most align with this direction are the same ones flagged discriminative by the statistical pipeline, that is a geometric explanation for why they are discriminative — they are not just statistically different, they literally encode the distinguishing concept as a directional axis.

Almost entirely free computationally — operates on existing CLS activations. Run via autoresearcher in background while D1 is the primary focus. Do not let D3 expand and compete with D1 for implementation time.

CT Cross-Generator Transfer Test Conditional Joint

Train classifier using only generator-A discriminative neurons, test on generator-B texts. Tests whether neurons that overlap across generators (D2a Jaccard) actually do the work across generators. The causal complement to D2a's correlational overlap measure.

Condition: Only if D1a + D2a are both complete by approximately April 18–20. Decide go/no-go at that checkpoint. The strongest single experiment in the paper if time permits.

04 — Experiment Plan

All Experiments in Detail

⬛ Step 0 — Foundation (Hard Blocker)

Run AUC-ROC + Mann-Whitney U + Bonferroni (α ≈ 3.25×10⁻⁷) for all 11 RAID generators. Threshold: AUC < 0.3 or > 0.7. Status: pipeline exists, not all generators complete. Owner: Paweł. Estimated: 0.5–1 day. Output: 11 discriminative neuron sets saved to disk.

Partial exception: D3a (mean difference vector) can run immediately on existing labeled CLS activations — start this now while Step 0 is in progress.

D1a Ablation Study (Zero + Mean) Primary Paweł

What & How

Zero ablation Set discriminative neuron values to 0. More disruptive — introduces out-of-distribution activations. Sanity check variant.
Mean ablation Set to the dataset mean activation per neuron. Keeps activations in-distribution. Primary reported variant.
Random baseline Ablate equal count of random neurons. 5 seeds, report mean ± std. Required — without this, any accuracy drop is uninterpretable.

Run per-generator (11 data points) then aggregate. Implement via forward-pass hooks on frozen BERT.

Possible Outcomes

A Neurons are necessary. Discriminative ablation drops accuracy significantly; random baseline does not. Expected. Contradicts the Enkhbayar paradox in a classifier setting. Main result of D1.
B Paradox holds — neurons are redundant. Discriminative and random ablation have similar impact. Surprising, still publishable. Detection signal is distributed; BERT compensates. Paper reframes around explaining the paradox.
C Generator-specific pattern. Necessary for some generators, redundant for others. Most nuanced. Connects to D2c — generator difficulty may predict whether neurons are causally necessary.
Effort~2 days

D1b Activation Patching Primary Paweł

What & How

Tests sufficiency. Take a human-text input, run forward pass, substitute discriminative neuron activations with values from a matched AI-text input. Observe whether the classifier flips to AI.

Matching: random pairing within same RAID domain. Report both continuous logit shift (preferred) and binary prediction flip.

Design decision: confirm matching strategy with Miłosz before implementing.

Possible Outcomes

A Reliable classifier flip. Patching discriminative activations reliably pushes logit toward AI. Neurons are sufficient. Combined with D1a necessity → double-sided causal claim. Ideal result.
B Weak or no flip. Logit does not shift reliably. Neurons not sufficient alone — detection requires context from other neurons. Qualifies the causal claim; report as distributed mechanism.
C Asymmetry with D1a. Necessary but not sufficient, or vice versa. Publishable nuance. Absence matters more than presence, or the reverse. Worth analyzing.
Effort~2–3 days
RequiresStep 0

D1c Integrated Gradients Primary Miłosz

What & How

Gradient-based attribution without modifying activations. Integrates gradient of classifier output w.r.t. neuron activations along path from zero baseline to actual input. Identifies which neurons most contributed to each specific classification decision.

Aggregate IG scores over AI-text and human-text samples separately. Then compare high-IG neurons against the AUC-flagged discriminative set from Step 0.

Possible Outcomes

A IG agrees with statistical pipeline. High IG-attribution ≈ AUC-flagged neurons. Cross-method validation. Two independent methods (distribution shift vs. gradient attribution) identify the same set. Strong methodological argument.
B IG and AUC diverge. Statistical neurons differ in distributions; IG neurons are causally active during decisions. Divergence means the model's "active" mechanism ≠ its statistically distinguishable neurons. Report and explain.
C IG agrees with D1a ablation but not AUC. Suggests ablation and IG are consistent causal measures; AUC captures correlational neurons that aren't causally active. Useful for the paper's methodological comparison.
Effort~2 days
RequiresStep 0 (for comparison)
Parallel withD1a, D1b
D2a Jaccard Similarity Matrix Co-primary Paweł

What & How

For each pair of the 11 RAID generators, compute Jaccard similarity: |A ∩ B| / |A ∪ B|. Produces an 11×11 symmetric matrix — the headline figure for D2. Visualize as a heatmap.

Additionally identify "core" neurons appearing in ≥N generator sets (e.g. ≥8 of 11) as candidates for a universal AI-detection signal. Nearly free once Step 0 is done — pure set arithmetic.

Possible Outcomes

A High Jaccard — large shared core. Universal mechanistic signal. BERT has learned a general detector. Pairs with CT: if shared neurons also transfer, the claim is strongly grounded.
B Low Jaccard — generator-specific. Explains why cross-generator detector transfer fails at the mechanistic level. Directly actionable interpretability finding.
C Family-clustered structure. High overlap within model families, low across. Most interesting. Mechanistic evidence that "AI-ness" is family-specific. Directly motivates D2c generator properties analysis.
Effort~1 day
RequiresStep 0 (all 11)
BlocksTransfer test CT
Output11×11 heatmap — key paper figure
D2b Layer Distribution Histogram Supporting Miłosz

What & How

For each generator, histogram of discriminative neuron counts by layer (1–12 for BERT-base-uncased). Compare distributions across generators. Replaces the invalidated U-shaped distribution claim from the old dataset.

Early layers encode syntactic patterns; late layers encode semantic/contextual information. The layer distribution tells you at what depth of representation the discriminative signal lives.

Possible Outcomes

A Consistent distribution across generators. Detection operates at the same representational depth regardless of generator. Characterize which layers dominate using Tenney et al.'s known layer-function mapping.
B Generator-specific layer patterns. Different generators leave traces at different levels of abstraction. Early = syntactic artifacts; late = semantic patterns. Most interesting outcome from D2b. Pair with D3b CAV-per-layer if that is run.
Effort~1 day
RequiresStep 0

D2c Generator Properties vs. Activation Distances Supporting Miłosz

What & How

Hypothesis: generators with similar properties activate similar neurons — their per-generator mean CLS activation vectors (restricted to discriminative neurons) will be geometrically close.

Method: compute mean CLS vector per generator → pairwise cosine distances → correlate with generator property distances.

Requires generator property table before implementation. Model size unavailable for GPT-4. Use: model family (OpenAI / Anthropic / open-source), instruction-tuned vs. base, open vs. closed weights.

Possible Outcomes

A Property distance correlates with activation distance. BERT's mechanism captures architectural properties of the generator. Explains why some generators are harder to detect. Strong supporting finding.
B No correlation with any tested property. Null result — still informative. Signal is content/domain-driven rather than model-architecture-driven.
C Distance pattern matches D2a Jaccard clustering. D2a and D2c tell the same story from different angles. Convergent validity — activation vector distance is the continuous version of discrete Jaccard overlap.
Effort~2 days
RequiresStep 0

D3a Mean Difference Vector + LR Weight Alignment Supporting Run now — no Step 0 needed

What & How

Mean diff vector Compute μ_AI and μ_human (mean CLS activations per class). d = μ_AI − μ_human. Rank neurons by |d_i| — their contribution to the AI-ness direction.
LR weight alignment Train logistic regression on full CLS activations. Weight vector w is the optimized AI-ness direction. Check: are high |w_i| neurons the same as AUC-flagged discriminative neurons? Measure via Spearman rank correlation between |w_i| and AUC scores, and top-k intersection.

Run immediately — operates on existing activations. ~1 hour.

Possible Outcomes

A Strong alignment — linear structure confirmed. Two independent methods (statistical AUC vs. geometric direction) identify the same neurons. Geometric explanation for why they are discriminative: they encode the AI-ness concept as a clean directional axis.
B Poor overlap — signal is distributed. AI-ness encoded in a subspace that cuts diagonally across neuron axes. Individual neuron analysis misses the structure. Mention as limitation; note DAS/DII as future work.
Effort~1 hour
RequiresNothing — run now

D3b CAV Per Layer Nice-to-have

What & How

Concept Activation Vector (TCAV, Kim et al. 2018). Train linear classifier at each of BERT's 12 layers on intermediate CLS activations. Weight vector is the CAV — linear direction for AI-ness at that depth. Requires new forward-pass hooks for all 12 layers.

Track CAV accuracy and alignment with per-layer discriminative neurons. Shows the evolution of the AI-ness representation through the network.

Possible Outcomes

A CAV accuracy increases with layer depth. AI-ness is a semantic concept, built up gradually. Consistent with BERT's known behavior.
B Separation emerges early and plateaus. Detection based on low-level statistical patterns — genuinely surprising given BERT's architecture.
C Generator-specific layer patterns. Convergent with D2b layer histograms — strong mutual reinforcement. Some generators syntactically obvious, others require deeper processing.
Effort~1–1.5 days
RequiresStep 0 + per-layer hooks
Reads withD2b layer histogram
CT Cross-Generator Transfer Test Conditional Joint

What & How

Train classifier using only generator-A discriminative neurons as features, test on generator-B texts. Use the D2a Jaccard matrix to select pairs: high-Jaccard pairs as positive test (do shared neurons transfer?), low-Jaccard pairs as negative control (does near-zero overlap predict near-chance accuracy?).

Possible Outcomes

A Transfer accuracy tracks Jaccard. High-Jaccard pairs → high transfer; low-Jaccard → near-chance. Causal generalization confirmed. D1 causality + D2a overlap + CT transfer = one coherent narrative. Strongest possible paper result.
B Transfer fails even for high-Jaccard pairs. Jaccard overlap is coincidental. Detection mechanism more entangled than neuron-level analysis suggests. Still publishable.
C Transfer works even for low-Jaccard pairs. A small undetected core is doing the work. Motivates identifying neurons present across all 11 generators as a separate analysis.
Effort~2–3 days
RequiresStep 0 + D1a + D2a

07 — Excluded Directions

What Is Not Being Pursued (and Why)

Clustering as Primary Analysis

Originally the paper's headline contribution. Dropped because: (a) clustering only discriminative neurons trivially produces exactly 2 clusters (AI vs human preference); (b) clustering the full feature space produces 1 dominant cluster of ~99% of neurons plus a small residual; (c) the three-cluster finding from wiki_labeled could not be reproduced on RAID. Hard to make clusters mean something — doesn't mean they naturally cluster.

Circuit Analysis

Identifying specific attention head × FF layer circuits. Excluded: TransformerLens is decoder-centric and encoder adaptation requires significant custom engineering; circuit analysis is a different research question from neuron-level causal validation; scope discipline for the May 25 deadline. Strong candidate for a follow-up paper.

Sparse Autoencoders (SAEs)

The dominant MI method for decoder-only models. No published SAE work on standard NLP BERT models exists as of early 2026. Architecture adaptation is substantial and out of scope. Follow-up paper territory.

Attention Head Analysis

Neurons are CLS token activations from the residual stream after both attention and FF processing — not from isolated attention heads. Attention head analysis is a different decomposition of the same signal. Not planned.

Classification Accuracy as Headline

98%+ accuracy with frozen BERT + sklearn is a known, non-novel result. The paper is about understanding what is happening inside an existing detector, not building a better one.

Minimal Pairs / Counterfactual Inputs

Deferred Contrastive inputs designed to flip neuron activations. Less mechanistic than ablation/patching; more behavioral. Deprioritized in favor of D1. Could appear as a qualitative supplement if D1 finishes early.

10 — Open Questions

Unresolved at Time of Writing

  • Does the Enkhbayar (2025) causal paradox — discriminative neurons being unnecessary for output — hold in a classifier setting? D1a and D1b together directly answer this.
  • Are the same neurons discriminative across all 11 RAID generators, or is there generator-specific specialization? D2a (Jaccard) characterizes this; either answer is publishable.
  • Is "AI-ness" encoded as a clean linear direction in CLS activation space? Do discriminative neurons define that direction? D3a answers the second part; the first is implicit in whether alignment is strong.
  • Do Miłosz's D2c generator properties correlate with activation distances? Requires the property table to be defined before this is testable.
  • Does the transfer test (CT) run at all? Contingent on D1a + D2a completing by approximately April 18–20.
  • Do D2b layer histograms and D3b CAV-per-layer converge on the same story about which layers carry the discriminative signal? Convergence here would be a strong supporting finding.
