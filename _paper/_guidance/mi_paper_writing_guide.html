SECTION 01

What Reviewers Actually Evaluate

Understanding the review criteria is the foundation of writing an accepted paper. ACL Rolling Review (ARR) evaluators score on two primary axes, plus several secondary dimensions.

S Soundness

Are claims backed by evidence? Is the methodology valid?

E Excitement

Is this novel? Would the community care? Is it interesting?

R Reproducibility

Can someone replicate this? Is code/data shared?

Review Score Breakdown (ARR Scale)

ScoreMeaningWhat It Takes
5Consider for AwardTop 2.5% — breakthrough insight, impeccable execution
4Main ConferenceClear contribution, sound methodology, novel findings
3.5FindingsSound work, incremental but valid contribution
3BorderlineSome merit but significant concerns
2ResubmitNeeds substantial revision
1RejectFundamental issues or out of scope
Key Insight Findings vs Main Conference: The primary criteria for Findings acceptance is soundness and reproducibility. Main conference papers must additionally demonstrate novelty, excitement, and/or impact. For a mechanistic interpretability paper, providing both correlational AND causal evidence is often what separates main track from findings.

Setup
~0.5p

~2p
→
Conclusion
~0.5p

+ Limitations (mandatory, not counted) + References (unlimited) + Appendix (optional)

The MI Paper DNA: What Accepted Papers Have in Common

Structure

Dual Evidence

Nearly all accepted MI papers provide both correlational (observation, probing, visualization) and causal (ablation, patching, intervention) evidence for their claims.

Scope

Multi-Model Validation

Top papers validate across 2–4 model families (e.g., Llama-2, Pythia, GPT-J). This addresses the "limited generalizability" concern preemptively.

Methodology

Mathematical Formalization

Every accepted paper includes formal notation for transformer internals (residual stream, attention outputs, FFN decomposition). This signals rigor.

Impact

Actionable Application

The best papers translate MI insights into a practical application: hallucination mitigation, knowledge editing, error correction, or bias reduction.

SECTION 03

Crafting a Compelling Title

Your title is the first filter. A strong MI paper title signals: (1) what mechanism is studied, (2) in what model, and (3) what approach is used. Accepted papers follow clear formulas:

The Three Dominant Title Patterns

PatternTemplateReal Example
Gerund + Target [Interpreting/Understanding/Analyzing] [Phenomenon] in [Model] "Interpreting Arithmetic Mechanism in LLMs through Comparative Neuron Analysis"
Mechanistic + Noun Mechanistic [Understanding/Analysis/Interpretation] of [Topic] "Mechanistic Understanding and Mitigation of Language Model Non-Factual Hallucinations"
Component-Level [Neuron/Circuit/Attention]-Level [Method] for [Goal] "Neuron-Level Knowledge Attribution in Large Language Models"
Tip Power words for MI titles: Mechanistic, Neuron-Level, Circuit, Causal, Attribution, Understanding, Interpreting, Tracing, Locating, Discovering, Internal, Unveiling. Avoid vague words like "Exploring" or "Investigating" alone — pair them with specifics.
SECTION 04

The 6-Sentence Abstract

The most effective MI paper abstracts follow a tight 6-sentence structure. Each sentence has a specific rhetorical function:

S1 — Context (What exists)

Establish the broad capability/problem. "State-of-the-art language models sometimes generate outputs that misalign with world knowledge."

S2 — Gap (What's missing)

Identify what we don't understand. "However, the internal mechanisms driving these [capabilities/failures] remain poorly understood."

S3 — This Paper (What we do)

State your contribution. "We present a comprehensive mechanistic analysis of [model] using [method], creating diagnostic datasets to trace [phenomenon]."

S4 — Key Finding (What we found)

Your main result. "We discover [N] distinct mechanistic causes: (1) [cause], and (2) [cause]."

S5 — Validation + Application

"Based on these insights, we propose [method] demonstrating superior performance over baselines."

S6 — Impact (Optional)

"Our analysis provides new insights into [broader topic] and sets the stage for [future direction]."

REAL ABSTRACT ANALYSIS — Yu et al., Findings EMNLP 2024
[S1 Context] "State-of-the-art language models (LMs) sometimes generate [text] that misalign with world knowledge." [S2 Gap] "To explore the mechanistic causes of these hallucinations..." [S3 Method] "...we create diagnostic datasets with subject-relation queries and adapt interpretability methods to trace hallucinations through internal model representations." [S4 Finding] "We discover two general and distinct mechanistic causes shared across LMs (Llama-2, Pythia, GPT-J): 1) insufficient subject attribute knowledge in lower layer MLPs, and 2) failure to select the correct object attribute in upper layer attention heads." [S5 Application] "Based on insights from our mechanistic analysis, we propose a novel hallucination mitigation method through targeted restoration of the LM's internal fact recall pipeline, demonstrating superior performance compared to baselines."
SECTION 05

Introduction: The 4-Move Structure

Every successful MI paper introduction follows four rhetorical moves. This structure is so consistent across accepted papers that deviating from it is risky.

Move 1 · ~2 paragraphs

Establish Territory

"Transformer-based large language models possess remarkable capabilities for [task], which is important for downstream tasks including [list]."

"While recent studies have made significant progress in understanding [area]..."

Move 2 · ~1 paragraph

Identify the Gap

"However, these studies do not provide insights into the internal mechanisms..."

"It is still hard to [goal] due to several reasons. Firstly,... Secondly,... Lastly,..."

Move 3 · ~1–2 paragraphs

Present Your Work

"In this paper, we focus on [level]-level [methods]. We analyze [aspect] and discover that [preview of finding]."

"Based on this finding, we employ [method] as [tool], enabling the identification of [target]."

Move 4 · Bulleted list

Enumerate Contributions

"Overall, our contributions are as follows:
a) We design...
b) We propose...
c) We analyze..."

Always 3–4 items. Each starts with a verb and includes a comparative claim.

Common Pitfall Don't oversell in the introduction. Reviewers explicitly flag "claims too strong" — use hedging: "Our results suggest..." rather than "Our results prove..." and "This provides evidence that..." rather than "This demonstrates that..."

Figure 1: The Pipeline Diagram

Nearly every accepted MI paper places a high-level overview figure in the introduction (or at the top of Section 3). This figure typically shows:

  • The model architecture being analyzed (simplified)
  • The specific components under investigation (highlighted)
  • The analysis pipeline (arrows showing methodology flow)
  • A preview of the key finding (e.g., which layers matter)

This figure is the single most important visual in the paper. Reviewers often decide their initial impression based on it.

SECTION 06

Related Work That Positions, Not Summarizes

The related work section should accomplish two things: (1) show you know the field deeply, and (2) clearly differentiate your contribution. It is NOT a literature review.

Typical Subsection Structure for MI Papers

SubsectionContentKey Citations
Attribution Methods Gradient-based, causal tracing, saliency methods — and their limitations Sundararajan et al. 2017; Meng et al. 2022; Vig et al. 2020
Mechanistic Interpretability Circuit discovery, superposition, dictionary learning, vocabulary projection Olah 2022; Elhage et al. 2021; Nanda et al. 2023; Bricken et al. 2023
[Your Specific Domain] The specific phenomenon you study (e.g., hallucinations, arithmetic, knowledge storage) Domain-specific citations
Positioning Phrase Templates Differentiation: "Unlike [citation] which focuses on [X], our work examines [Y] at [granularity] level."
Extension: "Building on the insights of [citation], we extend this line of work by [contribution]."
Gap identification: "While [citation] studied [aspect], the role of [your focus] remains unexplored."
Synthesis: "Our work bridges [line A] and [line B] by [how]."
SECTION 07

Methodology: Precision & Formalization

The methodology section is where MI papers win or lose. Reviewers in the Interpretability track expect mathematical precision.

The Three Pillars of a Strong Methods Section

Pillar 1

Formal Background

Begin with transformer architecture formalization. Define h^l_i, attention output A^l_i, FFN output F^l_i, and the residual stream. This is expected — not optional.

Pillar 2

Clear Definitions

Explicitly define key terms. E.g., "Definition of 'neuron': We regard the k-th column of W_o as the k-th attention subvalue..." Ambiguity kills papers.

Pillar 3

Step-by-Step Algorithm

Present your method as numbered steps or a pseudo-algorithm. Readers (and reviewers) should be able to reimplement from the description alone.

Standard Notation (used across accepted MI papers)

X = [t₁, t₂, ..., tₜ]     — Input token sequence
h^l_i                       — Hidden state at position i, layer l
A^l_i                       — Attention output at position i, layer l  
F^l_i                       — FFN output at position i, layer l
h^l_i = h^(l-1)_i + A^l_i + F^l_i   — Residual stream equation
y = softmax(E_u · h^L_T)   — Output probability distribution
W^q, W^k, W^v, W^o         — Query, Key, Value, Output projection matrices
α^l_{i,j,p}                — Attention weight (position p, head j, layer l)
m^l_{i,k}                  — FFN neuron coefficient score
Pro Tip Use the exact same notation as Geva et al. (2021) and Meng et al. (2022) for residual stream decomposition. Reviewers in this track are familiar with it, and using standard notation reduces cognitive load.

Experiments That Convince

The Experiment Design Funnel

Observation
What do we see?
→
Correlation
What patterns exist?
→
Causation
Does X cause Y?
→
Application
Can we exploit this?

The strongest MI papers follow this funnel exactly. Each experiment builds on the previous, creating a narrative arc from observation to application.

Experiment Types in Accepted MI Papers

Experiment TypePurposeExample
Information Flow Analysis Identify where information is processed Residual stream probing across layers; activation magnitude analysis
Activation Patching Causal verification of component roles Replace activations from clean run into corrupted run; measure effect
Ablation Studies Necessity testing — "Is this component needed?" Zero out specific neurons/heads; measure performance degradation
Steering / Intervention Sufficiency testing — "Can we control output?" Amplify/suppress specific neurons; demonstrate controlled behavior change
Cross-Model Comparison Generalizability of mechanisms Same analysis on Llama-2, Pythia, GPT-J shows consistent patterns
Pattern from EMNLP 2025 The paper "Interpretability Analysis of Arithmetic ICL" (Polyakov et al., EMNLP 2025) organized 7 activation patching experiments in a numbered table (Table 1), each testing a specific hypothesis. This makes the experimental logic crystal clear to reviewers and is an excellent structural pattern to follow.

Model Selection Best Practices

  • Minimum 2 model families for main track acceptance
  • Include at least one open-source model (Llama, Pythia, GPT-2) for reproducibility
  • Vary model sizes when possible (e.g., Pythia-410M, -1B, -6.9B)
  • Justify model choice: "We focus on [X] due to [availability / architectural properties / prior work comparability]"
SECTION 09

Results: Correlational + Causal Evidence

This is the critical differentiator. Providing only correlational evidence (probing, visualization) is the #1 reason MI papers get downgraded from Main to Findings or rejected entirely.

The Evidence Stack

Level 1 — Observation

Descriptive Statistics

Neuron activation distributions, attention weight heatmaps, layer-wise performance curves. Necessary but not sufficient.

Level 2 — Correlation

Probing & Projection

Linear probes, vocabulary space projection, PCA/UMAP visualization, statistical tests (AUC-ROC, Mann-Whitney U). Shows structure exists.

Level 3 — Causation

Interventions

Activation patching, ablation, neuron editing, causal mediation. This is what reviewers want.

Critical Rule For every correlational finding you report, include a causal validation experiment. The standard phrase is: "To verify the causal role of [component], we perform [intervention] experiments (Section X)."

Results Paragraph Template

TEMPLATE — RESULTS PARAGRAPH
"We observe that [finding] (Figure/Table N). [Brief interpretation — one sentence]. This finding is consistent with [citation] / Notably, this differs from [expectation]. To verify this observation, we perform [causal experiment] (Section N.M), which confirms that [intervention on component] leads to [quantified effect], providing causal evidence for [claim]."

Essential Phrases for Results Sections

  • "We validate our findings using correlational and causal evidence."
  • "Intervening on [N] neurons significantly [affects/changes] the final prediction."
  • "While numerous [units] contribute to [outcome], intervening on a few (N) is sufficient."
  • "Our results suggest that the model implements a [mechanism] that [description]."
  • "Taken together, these results indicate that [synthesis]."

Table Formatting Conventions

Standard Formatting Bold = best result in each column.
Underline = second best.
Use \midrule to separate baselines from your method.
Report mean ± std when running multiple seeds.
Always include a "Δ" or "Improvement" row/column showing gain over best baseline.

Accessibility Requirements

ACL mandates accessibility-conscious design:

  • Ensure grayscale readability — don't rely solely on color to convey information
  • Provide captions for every figure and table
  • Use patterns (dashes, dots) in addition to colors in line plots
  • Number figures and tables sequentially: "Figure 1:", "Table 1:"
SECTION 11

Limitations, Ethics & Mandatory Sections

ARR requires a Limitations section. Papers without one are desk-rejected. This section should be honest, specific, and frame limitations as future directions.

Limitations Template for MI Papers

TEMPLATE — LIMITATIONS SECTION
Model Scope: "Our analysis focused on [model family/size]; extending to [other architectures, e.g., Mamba, SSMs] and larger-scale models is an important direction for future work."

Task Generalizability: "We validated our findings on [N tasks/datasets]; the extent to which the identified mechanisms generalize to [broader settings] requires further investigation."

Methodological Caveats: "While we provide causal evidence through [patching/ablation], the complexity of modern LLMs means our analysis may not capture all relevant mechanisms. Polysemanticity and superposition may obscure [specific issue]."

Computational Constraints: "Due to the computational cost of [method], we limited our analysis to [scope]. Scaling to [larger scope] would require [specific resources]."
Pro Tip Frame each limitation as a clear future work direction. Reviewers appreciate honesty — listing real limitations actually increases perceived soundness.

SECTION 12

Pre-Submission Checklist

Use this checklist before every ARR submission. Each item maps to a specific reviewer concern.

Technical Soundness

  • Every claim has supporting evidence (figure, table, or formal argument)
  • Causal evidence provided for every correlational finding
  • Results validated across 2+ model families
  • Baselines include both classic and recent methods (5+ comparisons)
  • Statistical significance reported where applicable (p-values, confidence intervals)
  • Ablation study included showing each component's contribution

Novelty & Contribution

  • Clear differentiation from prior work stated explicitly
  • "To the best of our knowledge, this is the first..." claim is accurate
  • Contributions list has 3–4 specific items
  • At least one contribution is an actionable application (not just analysis)

Writing & Presentation

  • Abstract follows 6-sentence structure
  • Introduction uses 4-move structure
  • Figure 1 is a pipeline overview
  • All figures/tables have descriptive captions
  • Hedging language used for claims ("suggest", "indicate", "provide evidence")
  • No overclaiming or unsupported strong statements

Format & Compliance

  • Page limit respected (8 for long, 4 for short, excl. limitations & references)
  • ACL style files used without modification
  • Limitations section present
  • Responsible NLP checklist completed honestly
  • Anonymous submission (no self-identifying references, no named repos)
  • All references include DOIs or URLs where possible
  • Grayscale readability of all figures

Reproducibility

  • Code will be released (mention anonymized repo in paper)
  • Hyperparameters and training details fully specified
  • Dataset construction process documented
  • Compute budget mentioned (GPU type, hours)
  • Random seeds and number of runs reported
Final Thought The difference between a borderline paper and an accepted one is rarely a single breakthrough experiment. It is the accumulation of small signals of rigor: proper hedging, dual evidence, honest limitations, clean figures, and a narrative that flows from observation to mechanism to application. Every item on this checklist is one such signal.
